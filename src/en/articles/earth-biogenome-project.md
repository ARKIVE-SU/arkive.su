# Earth BioGenome Project: Sequencing Life for the Future of Life

**Why the complete genomic library of Earth must be preserved in the Knowledge Ark**

*Prepared for the Knowledge Ark Initiative (arkive.su). March 2026.*

## Introduction: Why Sequence All Life on Earth?

In 2018, the international scientific community launched one of the most ambitious projects in the history of biology: the **Earth BioGenome Project (EBP)** — an initiative to sequence, catalog, and characterize the genomes of all described eukaryotic species on the planet. This includes about 1.67 million species — from microscopic algae to blue whales, from soil fungi to thousand-year-old sequoias.

If the *Human Genome Project*, completed in 2003, decoded the genetic code of just one species at a cost of $6 billion, the EBP aims to do so for all eukaryotic life. The estimated cost is $3.9 billion, lower than initial projections thanks to the rapid reduction in sequencing technology costs.

The EBP is not a single laboratory but a "network of networks": over 2,200 scientists in 88 countries, connected through more than 61 affiliated projects. Coordination is managed from Arizona State University (secretariat) with the participation of the world's largest sequencing centers.

For the **Knowledge Ark Initiative**, EBP data represents exceptional value: it is a complete molecular blueprint of Earth's biosphere — a catalog of organizational solutions developed by evolution over 4 billion years. The loss of this data would be an irreparable loss for any future civilization trying to understand life, bioengineering, or restore lost ecosystems.

## Scale and Current Progress

The project is organized into three phases:
1. **Pilot Phase (2018–2020):** Established standards, ethical frameworks, and methodologies.
2. **Phase I (since 2021):** Aims to obtain a reference genome for each of the approximately 10,000 taxonomic families of eukaryotes.
3. **Phase II (through 2030):** Involves sampling 300,000 species and sequencing 150,000 of them, requiring the production of 3,000 reference genomes per month.

As of early 2025, EBP affiliated projects have released more than 3,300 high-quality genomic assemblies. Of these, 48% were produced directly by EBP affiliated projects — meaning the project is already generating nearly half of all high-quality eukaryotic genomes in the world.

## Largest Sequencing Centers

*   **Wellcome Sanger Institute** (Cambridge, UK) — the largest single producer of reference genomes. Through the *Darwin Tree of Life (DToL)* subproject, it systematically sequences British fauna and flora.
*   **BGI** (Shenzhen, China) — the second most powerful sequencing center with a global reach.

## Regional and Taxonomic Consortia

*   **European Reference Genome Atlas (ERGA)** — EBP's pan-European partner.
*   **Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP)** — aims for reference genomes of all vertebrate species.
*   **Africa BioGenome Project (AfricaBP)** — an initiative to sequence African biodiversity.

A full list of affiliated projects is available at [earthbiogenome.org/affiliated-project-networks](https://www.earthbiogenome.org/affiliated-project-networks).

## Technical Pipeline: From Field to Database

### Sample Collection
Reference sequencing requires fresh tissue. The standard procedure: a sample is frozen in liquid nitrogen (−196°C) and stored at −80°C. Each sample is assigned a **ToLID (Tree of Life Identifier)**.

### Sequencing
Current technologies include long-read sequencing on **Pacific Biosciences** (HiFi) and **Oxford Nanopore Technologies** platforms. **Hi-C** is used for scaffolding. The cost of a ~1 Gb reference genome is about $5,000 in direct costs.

### Data Deposition
All EBP data must be deposited in the **INSDC** — a trio of mirror databases: GenBank (NCBI), European Nucleotide Archive (ENA), and DNA Data Bank of Japan (DDBJ). Coordination and progress tracking are managed through **Genomes on a Tree (GoaT)**.

## Why EBP Data is Critically Important for Knowledge Ark

### Irreplaceability of Information
Species are going extinct. Every species lost before sequencing is an irrevocable loss of information developed over billions of years of evolution.

### Compactness and Self-Sufficiency of Data
A single species' genome ranges from tens of megabytes to several gigabytes. The entire EBP catalog (1.67 million species) could fit into several petabytes. The **FASTA** and **GFF3** formats are text files that any future civilization can read.

### Practical Value for Future Generations
A complete genomic library is:
*   The basis for synthetic biology and de-extinction.
*   A catalog of protein solutions for biotechnology (antibiotics, enzymes).
*   A training dataset for AI models (foundation models).

## Physical Storage of Biomaterials: Global Cryobanks

In addition to digital data, the preservation of physical materials plays a critical role:
*   **Frozen Zoo®** (San Diego Zoo, USA) — over 11,000 cell lines.
*   **Nature’s SAFE** (UK) — one of Europe’s largest living biobanks.
*   **Millennium Seed Bank** (Kew Gardens, UK) — a global bank for wild plant seeds.

## Conclusion

The Earth BioGenome Project is an attempt to create a complete digital library of eukaryotic life on Earth before it is lost. For the **Knowledge Ark**, EBP data is one of the highest archiving priorities. Preserving this data on ultra-durable media (ceramic, quartz) is a critical step in ensuring this knowledge remains available for future generations.
